# Welcome Content Library

Use these blocks to assemble a personalised welcome packet. Select and adapt each block based on the hire's role, location, and seniority. Avoid copying verbatim — personalise to the individual.

## 1. Opening welcome message

**Tone:** Warm, direct, human. Avoid "we are thrilled" and corporate superlatives.

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name],

Welcome to [Company]. We're glad you're here.

You're joining [team] at a genuinely interesting time — [one specific, relevant thing happening in the team or company right now, e.g., "we're shipping our first ML-native product feature" or "we've just closed our Series C and are doubling the engineering team"]. Your background in [relevant skill or experience from their profile] is exactly what we need for the road ahead.

This pack has everything you need to hit the ground running: your day-1 schedule, the tools you'll use, what your first 30 days look like, and a few things it took the rest of us a while to figure out.

See you on [start date].

[Hiring manager's name]
[Title], [Team]
```

Adapt the middle paragraph to be specific. Vague openers undermine the rest of the pack.

## 4. First 30 days and what success looks like

```
FIRST WEEK
- Get your tools set up and working (IT will help if anything's missing)
- Meet your immediate team
- Read through [team's Confluence space — link below]
- Shadow [buddy name] in at least one meeting or work session

WEEK 2–3
- Start picking up [first task or onboarding project — e.g., "your first support rotation", "a starter ticket in the backlog", "a shadowing session with a customer call"]
- 1:1s with your key collaborators (your manager will set these up)
- Share one observation or question in the team's Slack channel — we value fresh eyes

DAY 30 CHECK-IN
You and [manager name] will do a 30-day check-in. We'll talk about:
- What's clicked and what's still fuzzy
- Your first real contribution (whatever that looks like for your role)
- What support you need to go faster

There is no expectation of output in week 1. There is an expectation of curiosity.
```

## 6. Culture and team norms

Select the norms relevant to the hire's team. Keep it to 5–7 items — don't overwhelm.

```
A FEW THINGS WORTH KNOWING BEFORE YOU START

Async first. We default to writing things down rather than calling a meeting. If something can be a Slack message, it is. Confluence is where decisions live — not just outcomes, but why.

Meetings have agendas. If a meeting doesn't have one by 30 minutes beforehand, it's okay to ask for one or to decline. We try to protect maker time.

Feedback is direct but kind. We give it early and often — in 1:1s, in code reviews, in design crits. If something's not working, we say so. We expect the same from you.

Questions are welcome. Especially in the first 90 days. "I don't know" and "can you explain that?" are signals of intellectual honesty, not weakness.

No hero culture. We don't expect anyone to be on Slack at 10pm. If something's genuinely urgent, someone will reach out directly. Otherwise, protect your off-hours.

Camera on is the default for video calls, but not a rule. Read the room.

[Team-specific norm 1 — e.g., "We do a 10-minute team standup every morning at 9:30 on Slack (text, not video)."]
[Team-specific norm 2 — e.g., "Fridays are focus days — we avoid scheduling meetings after 12pm."]
```

## Output format

Assemble a single document in this order:
1. Opening welcome message (personalised)
2. Team and reporting line
3. Day-1 schedule (office or remote as appropriate)
4. First 30 days
5. Key tools and resources
6. Culture and norms (5–7 items, team-appropriate)

Keep the total document under 2 pages when printed. Cut anything generic. Every sentence should feel like it was written for this specific person.
